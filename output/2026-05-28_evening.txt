Tech stocks are looking worse than the dot-com bubble, and that's just where we're starting.

We're seeing a significant valuation correction rippling through the technology sector right now. The comparisons to nineteen ninety-nine are becoming increasingly common among market analysts, and there's a real sense that we're overdue for a serious pullback. The fundamentals don't match what investors have been willing to pay, and that gap is finally starting to close. Investors who've ridden these gains are facing tough decisions about whether to hold or take profits. What's particularly concerning is that unlike the late nineties, we're not seeing explosive growth in actual users or revenue to justify current price levels. The culprit here is the same thing that's driven every bubble: too much capital chasing too few opportunities with proven business models. And here's what matters most right now—this isn't a minor correction. When valuations revert to historical norms, it tends to hit hard and fast. So watch the next few weeks closely. If we break below key support levels, the panic selling could accelerate significantly.

In other news, the climate technology sector is heating up in the public markets. We've seen a wave of energy companies going public this year, and the momentum is real. Solv Energy, which focuses on solar and battery technology, went public in February and hit a six billion dollar valuation right out of the gate. Then X-Energy, the company building small modular nuclear reactors, followed suit in April, and its stock absolutely surged on day one. These aren't speculative startups either—these are companies with real technology, real customers, and real pathways to profitability. The reason this matters is simple: the world needs massive amounts of clean energy infrastructure, and the private markets alone can't fund it all anymore. These IPOs are sending a signal that climate tech has moved from hype to reality. Public markets are essentially saying these companies have solved enough of their engineering challenges that they're worth betting real money on. The outlook here is fascinating because we're likely to see more climate tech IPOs coming down the pipeline. The question isn't whether these companies will go public—it's how quickly the sector can scale and whether they can actually deliver on their promises while maintaining profitability.

Turning to artificial intelligence, there's a fascinating disconnect happening this graduation season. Former Google CEO Eric Schmidt recently spoke at the University of Arizona's commencement, telling graduates that their task is to help shape the future of AI. He was met with resounding boos. This moment captures something really important happening right now—the cultural perception of AI is shifting. Graduates, who represent a generation that's grown up with technology and understands its implications, aren't buying the techno-optimist narrative anymore. They see the labor concerns, the environmental costs, the questions about whose interests are really being served. Schmidt's vision of a world where young people should be excited to work on AI systems isn't resonating. This matters because it affects recruiting, it affects which problems entrepreneurs choose to tackle, and it affects the regulatory environment these companies will face. The hype around AI has been extraordinary, but you're seeing genuine skepticism starting to crack through the surface. Watch for more cultural pushback like this. It'll likely influence which AI companies attract top talent and which ones struggle to hire the people they need. The golden age of AI hype unchallenged might actually be ending.

On the corporate earnings front, Salesforce just reported first quarter twenty twenty-seven results, and there's clearly a lot happening in the enterprise software space right now. These earnings calls are telling us that companies are being extremely cautious about spending. They're looking for every efficiency gain they can find, which is putting real pressure on software vendors to prove their value. The theme across corporate America right now is about doing more with less, and that's reshaping how businesses think about their technology investments. Companies that can't clearly demonstrate ROI are facing headwinds. This feeds into broader market concerns about whether tech spending can maintain its current pace, especially as inflation and interest rates remain sticky.

AMD is signaling that its next wave in artificial intelligence is going to be substantially bigger than what we've seen so far. The company has been positioning itself as the alternative to NVIDIA in AI chips, and there's genuine momentum building there. AMD's architecture is finally reaching parity on performance while offering better pricing, which is starting to move the needle in customer conversations. Large hyperscalers are increasingly willing to diversify their chip suppliers, and AMD is the obvious beneficiary. This is significant because it could actually bring some competitive discipline to a market that's been dominated by one player. The next six months will be critical—we'll see whether major cloud providers actually start deploying AMD's latest generation chips in meaningful volume. If they do, AMD's business could grow dramatically.

Let's talk about energy markets. There's growing conviction among some market watchers that oil is about to experience a significant spike higher. The fundamentals are tightening—global demand remains strong, spare production capacity is limited, and there's geopolitical risk baked in. We've also seen OPEC maintain production discipline, which is supporting prices at elevated levels. The concern isn't about temporary spikes but about sustained higher prices that could stick around. This affects everything—inflation, corporate margins, consumer spending power. If oil does head higher as some are warning, it'll ripple through the entire economy. Watch crude prices and, more importantly, watch what energy companies do with their capital. If they're getting aggressive about expanding production again, that could cap upside. But if they remain cautious and disciplined, higher prices could be sustained.

In utility sector news, Spire just issued guidance that spooked investors pretty badly. When a utility company guides lower, it's usually because something meaningful has shifted in their outlook. Spire operates in a highly regulated space where earnings tend to be predictable, so when they surprise on the downside, it gets attention. The sell-off we've seen suggests the market is concerned about what this means for the broader utility sector. Utilities are supposed to be stable, defensive holdings, so any hint of trouble can trigger portfolio repositioning. This is one to watch because if other utilities start issuing similar warnings, it could signal broader economic stress. Keep an eye on the next earnings season in the utility space.

In international news, Japan Tobacco continues to hold steady. The company's been navigating a complex landscape of declining smoking rates in developed markets while maintaining significant revenue streams from legacy tobacco products and their diversified offerings. They're managing the transition reasonably well, which is why the stock has been stable. This isn't a growth story by any means, but it's a company that's handling a difficult secular transition better than many investors might expect. The dividends remain attractive, and management is being realistic about the headwinds they face. It's a good reminder that not every company needs to be a growth rocket to deliver value to shareholders.

Turning to cryptocurrencies, Bitcoin's situation is becoming increasingly complex. A significant Treasury operation worth one hundred fifty billion dollars is set to impact the market, and fund managers are warning that Bitcoin could actually be heading substantially lower. The macro backdrop is uncertain—interest rates, inflation expectations, and global risk sentiment are all in flux. When that kind of uncertainty combines with a large institutional operation, it can shake loose speculative positions. The risk here is that we see a cascade of liquidations if Bitcoin drops through key support levels. The one hundred fifty billion Treasury operation is essentially a wildcard that could trigger volatility in either direction. What's worth watching is whether institutions are actually buying these dips or whether they're taking profits. The behavior of smart money will tell you everything you need to know about where Bitcoin heads next. If institutions are stepping in to buy weakness, we might see a bounce. But if they're stepping aside, the downside could be more severe than recent price action would suggest.
