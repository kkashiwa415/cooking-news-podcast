Let's pause for a quick update on AI terminology, because if you've been nodding along to words like hallucinations, inference, or prompt engineering, it's worth actually knowing what those mean. Hallucinations are when an AI model confidently generates information that's completely false or made up. The system isn't being malicious; it's just producing text that sounds plausible but has no basis in reality. Inference is the process where an AI model takes in new information and generates a response based on what it learned during training. It's the active, real-time thinking part as opposed to the training part. Prompt engineering is the art of writing instructions to AI in a way that gets you the results you actually want. It's become a whole skill set because small changes in how you ask a question can dramatically change what the AI returns. There are others worth knowing: fine-tuning is when you take a pre-trained model and train it some more on your specific data to make it better at your particular problem. A token is basically a word fragment that the AI processes. Latency is how fast the model responds. These terms matter because they're shaping how people talk about AI capabilities and limitations. If you understand that hallucinations are a structural problem with how these models work, not just occasional glitches, you're going to have different expectations about where AI can be reliably deployed. That awareness is going to become increasingly important as AI gets embedded into more of our daily tools.

In other tech news, there's a serious warning coming from the security community about deploying large numbers of AI agents simultaneously. The concern is that as companies start automating more and more processes with AI agents, they're creating a complex web of interconnected systems where a single failure could cascade into major problems. Think about it: if you have AI agents making financial decisions, managing supply chains, controlling infrastructure, or executing trades, and those agents are all connected through APIs and databases, a bug in one system could trigger unintended behavior in another. The challenge is that AI agents operate in ways that aren't fully transparent or predictable, especially once you put multiple agents together. They can develop behaviors that weren't explicitly programmed. That's powerful when it's useful and terrifying when it's not. The security argument is that the industry is moving faster than our ability to properly test, validate, and contain these systems. There are no established circuit breakers or kill switches for misbehaving AI agents at scale. Companies are deploying agents because the potential productivity gains are massive, but they're doing it without the kind of infrastructure and safeguards that we'd normally expect for systems this powerful and interconnected. This is going to become a bigger issue before it gets resolved, and we're likely to see some public failures that force the industry to slow down and build better safety infrastructure.

Here's a concerning trend that's accelerating: companies are becoming what people are calling AI-pilled, meaning they're so convinced about AI's potential that they're making decisions about workforce and strategy without fully understanding the jobs they're trying to automate. The classic example is when leadership decides that AI can replace a customer service role without actually having spent time understanding everything that role involves. The people making these decisions are often removed from the work itself. ClickUp recently cut twenty two percent of its workforce to deploy AI agents instead. And this is happening at scale. Tech layoffs in twenty twenty six are already nearly matching all of twenty twenty five, even though we're only five months into the year. There's a psychological component here that people call AI psychosis, which is the disconnected confidence that AI can solve problems it probably can't yet solve. The danger is twofold. First, you might eliminate capabilities that are harder to replicate than you realized, and that comes back to hurt your product or service. Second, you're creating a job market crisis that's going to have real social consequences. The other concern is that once you've cut the experienced humans who understood the work deeply, it becomes much harder to fix whatever the AI missed. You lose the institutional knowledge. The companies that are moving more carefully, testing AI automation in specific areas before scaling it, are probably going to end up in better shape than those rushing to eliminate headcount. We're watching how quickly this mindset shifts once companies start experiencing actual negative consequences from poorly deployed AI automation.
