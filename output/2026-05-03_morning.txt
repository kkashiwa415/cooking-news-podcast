Now let's turn to transportation, where cities are grappling with a pretty fundamental question: how do you give a ticket to a self-driving car? Robotaxis are already operating in multiple cities, but the legal and operational frameworks haven't quite caught up. If a robotaxi violates traffic laws, runs a red light, or gets into an accident, who's responsible? The car's obviously not going to respond to a traffic stop. You can't exactly ask the vehicle to pull over and show you its insurance. Cities are wrestling with whether citations go to the registered owner, the robotaxi company, the passenger, or some combination. This is affecting everyone from city governments trying to enforce traffic laws to robotaxi operators to insurance companies to regular drivers who need to know what the rules of the road actually are. It matters because without clear frameworks, you either end up with robotaxis operating in legal gray zones or cities banning them outright. Some jurisdictions are creating new protocols—maybe ticketing the fleet operator, maybe having the car automatically register violations, maybe assigning liability based on who had control at that moment. Watch for standardized frameworks to emerge over the next year or two, and expect this to become a template issue for other autonomous vehicle regulations.
