Shifting to corporate strategy and innovation, there's an important lesson emerging about how large organizations can actually derive value from artificial intelligence. Most big companies approach new technology by starting with what the tech can do and then trying to find applications for it. It's a common pattern—you've got a new hammer, so you look for nails. But the research suggests this approach captures less than one-third of the potential value that digital investments should deliver. The companies that actually break through and achieve breakthrough AI innovation work differently. They start with customer needs and work backward to the technology. They ask what problems their customers actually face, what outcomes would genuinely improve the customer experience, and then they build AI solutions specifically designed to solve those problems.

This seems obvious in theory, but in practice it requires a different organizational structure and decision-making process. It means the engineering team takes direction from customer research rather than the other way around. It means accepting that some of the most powerful AI capabilities your engineers can build might not actually matter to your customers. It means slowing down to understand context before accelerating with solutions. Companies that have done this successfully report much higher ROI on their AI investments and faster time to actual business value. The takeaway for investors and business leaders is that if you're evaluating a company's AI strategy, pay attention to whether they're customer-back engineering or technology-forward experimenting. The difference between those two approaches will increasingly determine which companies actually capture value from artificial intelligence and which ones spend millions building impressive technology that nobody actually wants to use.
