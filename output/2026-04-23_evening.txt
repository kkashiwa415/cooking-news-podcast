Plastic buildings might soon stop being a punchline and start being a solution to two massive global problems at once.

MIT engineers have figured out how to turn single-use plastics that would otherwise end up in landfills into a viable construction material. The research team, led by mechanical engineering professor David Hardt, found a way to process discarded plastics into components that can be used in home construction. This addresses two urgent pressures simultaneously: the environmental burden of plastic waste that just keeps piling up, and the housing shortage facing a growing global population. The process takes materials that are basically worthless today and transforms them into building components that can actually compete with traditional materials like timber. The implications here are pretty significant. Construction is resource-intensive, and anything that reduces pressure on forest ecosystems while simultaneously solving the plastic waste crisis is worth paying attention to. The next phase will be scaling this from the lab into actual production and testing how these plastic-based structures hold up over decades. If the durability checks out, you could see this technology becoming part of mainstream construction within the next several years.

In other news on the science front, researchers at MIT have identified a natural protein that appears to provide broad-spectrum protection against harmful bacteria in the gastrointestinal tract. The protein is called intelectin-2, and it sits embedded in the body's mucosal surfaces where it binds to sugars found on cell surfaces. What makes this discovery interesting is that intelectin-2 does double duty: it both helps fortify the mucosal barrier itself and offers protection against a range of bacterial infections. The protein essentially recognizes specific sugar patterns that bacteria carry and neutralizes them before they can establish an infection. This affects anyone concerned about gut health and gastrointestinal infections, which is basically everyone. For patients dealing with chronic GI issues, this could eventually lead to new therapeutics. The research is preliminary, but if scientists can figure out how to boost intelectin-2 levels or deploy it therapeutically, you're looking at a potential treatment path for infections that currently require antibiotics. The researchers are now working on understanding exactly how to translate this finding into clinical applications.

Turning to neuroscience, scientists believe they've found a tool that could finally help us understand how consciousness actually works. The technology is called transcranial focused ultrasound, and it's noninvasive, which is huge because exploring consciousness has historically required brain surgery. MIT philosopher Matthias Michel and colleagues from Lincoln Lab have outlined a strategy for using this ultrasound technology to study the relationship between physical matter in the brain and subjective experience—thoughts, sensations, and emotions. The beauty of this approach is that you can actually stimulate specific regions of the brain without cutting into it, and you can do it repeatedly in ways that would be impossible with surgery. This matters because consciousness remains one of the biggest unsolved puzzles in science. We still don't really know how the physical processes happening in your three pounds of brain matter translate into the experience of being you. With this new tool, researchers might finally be able to map that relationship more directly. The next step is running actual clinical trials and seeing whether focused ultrasound can reliably trigger measurable changes in conscious experience. If it works as hoped, we could see breakthroughs in understanding consciousness within the next few years.

In cannabis, Green Thumb Industries is making a significant strategic bet on a regulatory and tax issue. The company is gambling on changes to Section 280E of the tax code, which currently prevents cannabis businesses from deducting business expenses on their federal taxes. This is a huge structural disadvantage compared to other industries. If 280E were to be repealed or reformed, Green Thumb's tax burden would drop substantially, which would be enormously accretive to earnings. The company is essentially positioning itself to benefit if there's a regulatory shift toward cannabis normalization. This is a medium-term bet, not something happening next quarter. The change would require congressional action, so it's dependent on the political environment and whether cannabis reform gets prioritized. For investors in Green Thumb, this represents optionality—if 280E changes, there's significant upside. If it doesn't, the company still operates its business, but without that tax benefit.
