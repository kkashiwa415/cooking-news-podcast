# EVENING EDITION NEWS BRIEFING

The Musk versus OpenAI trial just entered week two, and things are getting personal up there in the courtroom.

Let's dive into that first. Elon Musk took the stand last week alleging that Sam Altman and Greg Brockman, OpenAI's CEO and president, basically deceived him into donating thirty-eight million dollars back when the company was still a nonprofit. Musk claimed they promised to keep the organization noncommercial, and that promise was broken. Well, this week OpenAI fired back with some pretty damaging testimony. Shivon Zilis, who sits on Musk's board at X and has ties to this whole saga, revealed something pretty striking: Musk actually tried to poach Sam Altman away from OpenAI for his own artificial intelligence venture. That's a pretty significant detail in this case because it reframes the whole motivation behind the lawsuit. If Musk was trying to recruit Altman to work for him, the court's going to have to consider whether this suit is really about breach of contract or whether it's got other motivations underneath. The implications here are huge for both men and for the AI industry watching this unfold. What you're seeing play out is the messiness of how the AI race actually works behind closed doors. Altman and Musk were once aligned, and now they're at each other's throats in court. The outcome here could set precedent for how nonprofit organizations that convert to for-profit structures are held accountable to their early donors. Keep an eye on the next round of testimony because this trial's shaping up to be a real window into the power dynamics of Silicon Valley.

In other news, a former cybersecurity executive just got hit with a massive judgment for his role in selling sensitive government hacking tools to the Russians. Peter Williams worked at a US defense contractor and managed to steal several surveillance and hacking tools before selling them for about one point three million dollars to a Russian broker with ties to Putin's government. The court ordered him to pay ten million dollars in damages to his former employers. This case really highlights how vulnerable our defense infrastructure is when insiders decide to betray their country for profit. Williams had access to sophisticated tools that could've compromised national security, and he essentially handed them over to a hostile foreign power. Everyone's affected by this, frankly, because it's a reminder that the threat doesn't just come from outside hackers trying to break in—it comes from people on the inside who have trusted access. What matters here is that it shows the government is taking these breaches seriously and willing to pursue massive financial penalties to send a message. The outlook on this is clear: expect more prosecutions of insider threats, and expect corporations and defense contractors to tighten their security protocols around who has access to what.

RGA's Q1 results paint a picture of how well the insurance markets are holding up more broadly. These companies are always watching mortality rates, natural disaster patterns, and market conditions because all of that flows through to their bottom line. For investors in the insurance space, RGA's results matter because they're a bellwether for the whole reinsurance industry. The move up today came alongside broader positive sentiment in the crypto markets, though specific catalysts aren't entirely clear. And finally, there's a bigger-picture story developing in the crypto regulatory space. Stablecoins just got what's being called their permission slip—essentially regulatory clarity that they're allowed to operate under a certain framework. But getting the green light is only half the battle. The hard part is actually building the infrastructure and getting institutional adoption once you know the rules of the road. Stablecoins are critical to crypto because they're supposed to provide price stability for people who want exposure to blockchain without riding the bitcoin and ethereum roller coaster. For way too long, the regulatory status of stablecoins has been murky, which has made it hard for mainstream financial institutions to embrace them. Now that clarity's starting to emerge, expect to see more traditional finance entities dip their toes in. But the real test is whether stablecoin issuers can actually build the reserve infrastructure, get the right banking partners, and create enough liquidity that they become genuinely useful for everyday transactions. What to watch is adoption rates over the next few quarters and whether we start seeing major payment networks and traditional banks actually start to integrate stablecoins into their systems.
