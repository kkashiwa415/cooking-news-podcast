# Evening News Briefing – Saturday, May 23, 2026

Kevin Warsh just took over the Federal Reserve, and markets are already recalibrating in real time.

Let's start with what's happening at the Fed. Warsh's appointment represents a notable shift in monetary policy direction. He's known for a more market-friendly stance compared to his predecessors, and traders wasted no time pricing in what that could mean. Bitcoin, which has been treading water near seventy-seven thousand dollars, dipped lower on Friday as investors processed the leadership change. The broader implications are still unfolding, but the market's reaction tells you something important: this isn't a continuation of the status quo. Warsh has historically favored a lighter regulatory touch and a more accommodative approach to financial conditions. For investors holding risk assets, this could offer some tailwind. For those worried about inflation, the calculus gets trickier. His leadership will likely shape everything from interest rate trajectories to how aggressively the Fed intervenes in markets. The real test comes in his first policy meetings and how he calibrates the Fed's response to what's already a complicated economic picture.

Speaking of that complicated picture, flash PMI data just came in, and it's painting a troubling portrait. Major developed economies are showing signs of stagflation—that particularly nasty combination of slowing growth paired with stubborn price pressures. Growth is faltering across the board while inflation remains elevated. This puts central banks in an impossible position. Cut rates too aggressively and you risk letting inflation gain ground. Hold steady and you risk deepening an economic slowdown. Companies are facing margin pressure from rising input costs while demand growth stalls. Workers are seeing their wage gains eroded by persistent price increases. Consumers are caught in the middle, trying to stretch paychecks while businesses grapple with uncertainty about whether they can pass costs along or have to absorb them. This backdrop matters enormously for equity valuations, earnings forecasts, and policy decisions ahead. If stagflation takes hold, it typically favors certain defensive sectors over others, and we're already seeing that play out.

Energy stocks, in particular, are being viewed as one of the last hedges standing in this environment. While traditional safe havens like government bonds aren't offering much yield, energy provides both exposure to inflation dynamics and the potential for meaningful dividends. Oil prices remain supported by geopolitical tensions and steady global demand, even as growth slows. Producers aren't ramping up supply as aggressively as they might in a healthier economy, so the supply-demand balance stays relatively tight. For portfolio managers looking to hedge stagflation without going completely defensive, energy offers a way to participate in inflation protection while still owning something with economic sensitivity. That calculus is likely to keep energy as a meaningful part of allocations through the second half of the year.

In other news, there's trouble brewing in the chemicals sector. AdvanSix, a major chemical producer, just saw its target cut by analysts due to mounting input cost pressures. The company manufactures specialty chemicals used across manufacturing, agriculture, and industrial applications, and it's getting squeezed from both sides. Raw material costs remain elevated, and the company's struggled to pass all of those increases through to customers. Competition makes price increases difficult, even when your own costs spike. The downgrade signals that the margin compression affecting many industrial companies isn't temporary—it's proving sticky. AdvanSix specifically faces challenges where it can't fully offset higher inputs with higher prices, meaning actual earnings will come in lighter than hoped. This is a microcosm of a broader theme: companies that process or handle commodities as inputs are getting caught in a vice. For investors in the industrial and chemical space, this is a signal to look carefully at pricing power and customer concentration before committing capital.

Turning to some of the infrastructure plays that are supposed to benefit from long-term secular trends. Amphenol, a major connector and cable manufacturer, is being positioned as the pick-and-shovel play for the artificial intelligence and robotics boom. The idea's straightforward: whether you're building data centers, industrial robots, autonomous systems, or the next generation of computing infrastructure, you need connectors and high-speed data transmission equipment. Amphenol makes the products that enable all of that. Unlike software companies that might face winner-takes-most dynamics, Amphenol benefits from proliferation—more infrastructure gets built, more connectors get sold. The company's been quietly gaining share as customers shift away from older, lower-speed legacy connectors to newer, higher-speed ones. That transition is still playing out across multiple customer segments. The appeal here is that you're getting exposure to the AI and robotics megatrends without the venture capital-like volatility of actual AI companies. Whether it's data center buildouts, electric vehicle assembly, or factory automation, Amphenol has a piece of the puzzle. Given the stagflation backdrop, investors are increasingly drawn to companies with steady demand drivers and operational leverage, rather than speculative bets on software or services. Amphenol fits that profile.

Now, hotel real estate has its own compelling value story right now. Pebblebrook Hotel Trust, a hospitality real estate investment trust specializing in upscale and premium hotels, is being highlighted as a value candidate. The broader hotel sector got hammered during recent economic uncertainty, with valuations compressing significantly. But travel demand, particularly from international visitors and higher-income travelers, has actually remained resilient. Pebblebrook's portfolio is positioned in markets that cater to affluent leisure and business travelers—properties that command higher rates and boast strong operational fundamentals relative to the broader industry. As interest rates stabilize under the new Fed leadership, the financing environment for real estate becomes more predictable. REITs that were trading at deep discounts to underlying asset values are starting to look attractive again. Pebblebrook's particular advantage is its focus on premium properties with pricing power. Even in a stagflation environment, luxury travel tends to hold up better than budget alternatives. The dividend yield combined with potential asset appreciation as valuations normalize makes it interesting for income-focused investors willing to weather near-term volatility.

Shifting gears to the technology side, Spotify's making a big push into AI tools, and the move is raising some eyebrows. The streaming giant has released a suite of AI-powered features that encourage users to create more content—playlists, podcasts, personalized mixes, that sort of thing. The underlying strategy makes business sense: more content creation equals more engagement, more time on platform, and more data to feed advertising algorithms. The problem is execution. Users are increasingly irritated by the relentless nudging toward content creation they didn't ask for. It's giving algorithm-driven feature creep, where the service starts optimizing for metrics that don't align with what listeners actually want. The fundamental tension is this: Spotify's trying to be both a passive consumption platform where you discover music and an active creation platform where you're constantly prompted to make something new. Most users came for the former and didn't sign up for the latter. There's a real risk that heavy-handed AI recommendations and creation prompts make the core experience worse, not better. For Spotify, the challenge is balancing engagement metrics with user satisfaction. For competitors, this is an opening: a simpler, less noisy experience could actually be appealing if they execute it well.

On the regulatory front, there's an emerging collision between the Trump administration and technology researchers. Tech researchers studying online safety and hate speech are suing the administration over what they see as an assault on research itself. The argument goes like this: researchers need to understand how harmful content spreads and how to counter it, and that requires some ability to study the problem without government interference. The Trump administration's approach has been to go after researchers and the institutions funding their work, effectively chilling research in this space. From the administration's perspective, many researchers are pursuing ideological agendas disguised as objective study. From the researchers' perspective, you can't solve a problem if you can't study it. This is becoming a serious issue because it affects everything from content moderation standards to platform accountability. If researchers can't openly investigate online safety issues, platforms lose an important external check on their own safety practices. Conversely, if research becomes openly adversarial with government, it loses credibility and influence. The real stake here is whether we have a functioning ecosystem where independent experts can contribute to policy and platform decisions, or whether everything becomes purely ideological. This litigation will likely rumble through courts for a while, but it signals a broader breakdown in how technology policy gets made.

Looking at the crypto markets specifically, Bitcoin's momentum has stalled around seventy-seven thousand dollars. The Warsh Fed leadership transition is part of the reason—uncertainty about how aggressively the new Fed will support financial conditions. Bitcoin tends to rally when investors expect loose monetary policy and stumble when the Fed signals a more restrictive stance. Warsh's appointment was initially seen as potentially positive for crypto since he's more market-friendly, but markets have been repricing that narrative as uncertainty sets in. The broader crypto picture also depends on broader macro conditions. In a stagflation environment, crypto becomes trickier to position. It should theoretically benefit from inflation hedging demand, but it also tends to sell off when growth worries intensify because it's still a risk asset. Institutional adoption continues to expand, but retail interest has cooled. The path forward likely depends on how the Warsh Fed's first few months of policy play out and whether stagflation fears ease or intensify.

So here's what to watch: the Fed's first major policy announcement under Warsh's leadership, earnings reports in coming weeks to see how badly margin compression is hitting, data on whether stagflation persists or stabilizes, and the trajectory of that online safety litigation. These threads are all connected—Fed policy affects capital allocation, stagflation affects earnings, and regulatory uncertainty affects which companies investors are willing to own. Stay tuned.
