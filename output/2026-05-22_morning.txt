SpaceX just launched its upgraded Starship V3 for the first time, and it was mostly a win—except for that whole booster problem.

The company successfully flew the next generation of its massive rocket today, marking a significant milestone in its development timeline. The V3 represents a major upgrade from previous versions and is essential to SpaceX's long-term plans, including lunar missions, Mars exploration, and eventually making humanity multiplanetary. But here's the catch: while the main ship performed well overall, the booster didn't make it back in one piece. The team lost it during the return phase, which is a setback for their goals of achieving rapid reusability. Still, a mostly successful first test flight keeps SpaceX on track with its ambitious roadmap. The company's been pushing hard to iterate quickly and improve each generation, so losing a booster on the maiden flight isn't ideal, but it's not catastrophic either. Folks in the space industry are already watching to see how SpaceX adjusts the design for the next attempt.

Speaking of Mars ambitions, there's an interesting twist on who's actually going. The founder of F2Pool, one of the world's largest mining pools that controls about eleven percent of Bitcoin's total computing power, has been selected to lead SpaceX's first crewed mission to Mars. This is a genuinely unexpected combination of cryptocurrency and space exploration. The selection highlights how the lines between different industries are blurring, and it also speaks to the sheer amount of wealth and influence concentrated in the crypto mining space. F2Pool's founder will reportedly lead mission planning and operations for this historic journey. It's a major vote of confidence from SpaceX, and it's bound to make waves in both the crypto community and the space world. We'll be watching to see how this partnership develops as actual mission timelines start to firm up.

In other space news, Blue Origin just got the green light to fly its New Glenn mega rocket again after an engine failure last month. The company had suffered a significant setback in April when an engine malfunction destroyed an AST SpaceMobile satellite that was scheduled for launch. Blue Origin confirmed that diagnosis publicly today, though they haven't released extensive technical details about what exactly went wrong. The fact that they've been cleared to resume operations is important for Jeff Bezos' company and for the broader commercial space market. Blue Origin is critical infrastructure for satellite launches and other heavy-lift missions, so any extended downtime is costly for customers. The company's working through its investigation process methodically, and regulators appear satisfied that it's safe to proceed. Expect New Glenn to return to the pad in the coming weeks as Blue Origin works to rebuild customer confidence and get back on schedule.

Robinhood has been one of the more mainstream-friendly platforms for crypto trading, so this departure signals some strain in that market segment. Her exit suggests the company may be recalibrating its strategy or facing headwinds in user growth and transaction volumes. The crypto trading space has always been volatile, and Robinhood's traditional stock brokerage business has been its bread and butter. This move might indicate they're prioritizing other areas of their platform. Look for announcements about her replacement and any strategic shifts in how Robinhood approaches crypto going forward.

Back to crypto policy now. An SEC Commissioner is pushing back against concerns that a new crypto rule will inadvertently create problems for the market. Commissioner Peirce is defending a recent regulatory proposal against claims that it could foster the growth of synthetic tokens that might destabilize the market. The concern from critics is that certain provisions could be exploited to create duplicative or risky token structures. Peirce's counterargument is that the rule is designed thoughtfully and that the fears are overblown. This is a significant debate within the SEC, which has been split on crypto policy. Peirce has historically been the most crypto-friendly commissioner, so her defense carries weight among industry supporters. The outcome of this debate will shape how digital assets are treated under U.S. law going forward. Watch for formal votes on this rule and see which way other commissioners lean.

In venture capital news, there's a growing awareness that some AI startups are playing games with their revenue numbers. Specifically, founders and their investors are inflating annual recurring revenue figures—what people call ARR—to make early-stage AI companies look more impressive than they actually are. What makes this particularly interesting is that VCs are often fully aware this is happening. They're essentially winking at the practice because the hype machine around AI is so profitable right now. The logic goes like this: if you can show sky-high ARR numbers, you attract bigger funding rounds at higher valuations, which makes existing investors look great on paper. It's a version of the old playbook we've seen before in tech bubbles—optimistic accounting, aggressive revenue recognition, and a collective willingness to not ask too many hard questions. The concern, of course, is that when these companies actually need to deliver real profits, a lot of them will disappoint. This is a cautionary tale about how even sophisticated investors can get caught up in hype cycles and start rationalizing practices they'd normally flag immediately.

Minnesota is taking an interesting approach to crypto by empowering local banks to compete for this business. The state's enabling smaller regional banks to offer crypto services, potentially giving them a better chance to compete against Wall Street's big players who've been moving aggressively into digital assets. Local and regional banks have typically been shut out of the crypto space due to regulatory complexity and capital requirements. Minnesota's move is designed to level the playing field and keep crypto revenue flowing to smaller financial institutions rather than concentrating it all at JPMorgan and Bank of America. This reflects a broader recognition that crypto is becoming a meaningful part of financial services, and there's no good reason to restrict it only to the largest institutions. It's also a bit of economic development strategy—keeping financial services jobs and revenue in the state rather than seeing them drain to New York or San Francisco. Other states will probably be watching this closely to see if it works.

Now here's a cybersecurity story with some unexpected characters involved. Kash Patel's clothing brand website got hacked and is now shut down. According to reports on social media, hackers infiltrated the site and used it to try to trick visitors into installing malware. It's unclear exactly how extensive the breach was or what personal information might have been compromised. The hackers appear to have redirected traffic and created fake download prompts to spread the malicious code. This is a good reminder that even websites run by prominent political figures aren't immune to basic cybersecurity failures. It's also a reminder that you should always be suspicious of unexpected download prompts on websites, no matter how legitimate they might look. Patel's team has since taken the site offline while they investigate and presumably work with security experts to understand what happened and rebuild securely.

Moving to crypto regulation now, Congressman Tom Emmer continues pushing his Clarity Act despite pushback from law enforcement. The legislation is designed to provide clearer rules for crypto, which proponents argue will help the industry grow. But law enforcement agencies have raised concerns that the bill could make it harder to track and prosecute financial crimes and money laundering. Emmer is brushing off these objections, arguing that good regulation and law enforcement aren't mutually exclusive. This is a genuine policy debate with real tradeoffs. Law enforcement wants broad discretion to investigate financial crimes, while industry wants bright-line rules so they know exactly what's legal. The Clarity Act sits somewhere in the middle, but law enforcement feels it swings too far toward industry. Emmer's stance suggests this bill has legs in Congress and will likely move forward despite the concerns. This is something to monitor as it winds through the legislative process.

Apple's mounting a Supreme Court challenge to Epic's big win. The iPhone maker is asking the highest court to overturn or narrow the App Store injunction that Epic Games won in lower courts and to eliminate a contempt ruling over external payment links. Epic's victory was massive—it gave app developers more freedom to process payments outside Apple's system and avoid the company's thirty-percent commission. Apple's arguing that one lawsuit shouldn't reshape rules for the entire App Store ecosystem. The company is essentially saying that the remedy shouldn't be so broad, even if Epic proved anticompetitive conduct. This is a fascinating legal moment because App Store policies affect millions of developers and billions of users. If Apple wins at the Supreme Court, it could largely restore its previous power. If Epic's victory stands, it fundamentally changes how app distribution works. The Supreme Court will need to balance competition policy against Apple's property rights. Expect this to drag on for a while, but the stakes couldn't be higher for the tech industry.

Finally, Congress is launching a serious investigation into prediction markets. Polymarket and Kalshi, two platforms that let people bet on real-world events, are being probed over concerns that government employees might be trading on inside information. The specific worry is that federal workers with access to nonpublic information could be using these platforms to profit from advance knowledge of policy decisions or market-moving announcements. It's a pretty serious allegation that touches on market integrity and government ethics simultaneously. These prediction markets have exploded in popularity and are now influencing how people think about future events. If they're compromised by insider trading, that's a major problem. Congress is asking tough questions about how these platforms verify user identities and whether they have adequate safeguards against this kind of abuse. The platforms will need to either demonstrate robust compliance measures or face potential regulatory crackdowns. This could shape how prediction markets are regulated going forward and whether they're seen as legitimate information aggregators or risky vehicles for insider trading.
