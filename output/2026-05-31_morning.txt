Now let's talk about something that keeps economists up at night. International countries have only purchased about ten percent of all new U.S. government debt issued over the past eighteen months. That's a significant decline from historical norms, and it signals a real problem on the horizon.

For decades, foreign governments and investors — especially countries like China and Japan — have been reliable buyers of American Treasury bonds. They hold them as reserves, they find them safe, and it's been a steady demand source that helps keep interest rates manageable. But that appetite is drying up. When foreign demand for Treasury debt drops, domestic demand has to fill the gap, and that puts upward pressure on yields. Higher yields mean higher borrowing costs for the government, which eventually flows through to everything else in the economy — mortgages get more expensive, car loans get more expensive, business borrowing gets more expensive.

The reasons are varied. Some countries are facing their own fiscal challenges and can't afford to park as much money in foreign assets. Others are diversifying away from dollar holdings as a hedge against geopolitical risk. And frankly, some are just choosing to lend to each other rather than prop up American deficits. The real concern is whether this trend accelerates. If foreign demand continues to shrivel, the government will need to attract more domestic buyers, which means offering higher returns. That works for a while, but it creates a dynamic where debt service becomes an ever-larger piece of the budget. Keep an eye on Treasury auction results — if foreign bidding continues to weaken, we'll see it show up in the data, and that's when markets will really start to worry.
