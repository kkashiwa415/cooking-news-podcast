SpaceX just revealed its IPO filing, and it's packed with more ambitious bets than you'd expect from a rocket company.

The company finally went public with the details weeks ahead of what's shaping up to be the largest IPO in history. The filing shows SpaceX is doubling down on AI investments while also charting ambitious plans for Starship and establishing a unique corporate structure where Elon Musk will simultaneously serve as CEO, CTO, and Chairman of the board. That level of consolidated control is pretty unusual for a company of this scale, but SpaceX is betting that having Musk in all three roles will let them move faster on their core moonshot projects while pursuing new frontiers in artificial intelligence.

The company is also holding a significant crypto position. SpaceX had eighteen thousand seven hundred twelve bitcoin in its holdings as of the end of the first quarter, valued at one point two nine billion dollars. That's not just pocket change—it represents a deliberate bet on Bitcoin's staying power, and it gives us a window into how Musk's companies are allocating capital across different asset classes.

But here's where it gets really interesting on the AI front. The filing reveals just how much SpaceX's sister company xAI is spending. xAI burned through six point four billion dollars last year alone while working on expanding Grok and building out massive data center infrastructure. And they're not slowing down. xAI has committed to buying two point eight billion dollars worth of natural gas turbines over the next three years to power its data centers. The company is already facing lawsuits over its current generators, but it's pressing ahead with even larger power infrastructure investments. That's a massive real-world constraint that most AI startups don't have to worry about—you can't run cutting-edge AI without serious electricity, and securing that supply is becoming one of the biggest bottlenecks in the industry.

Speaking of xAI's compute, the filing also revealed a stunning partnership detail. Anthropic, one of the leading AI safety companies, has agreed to pay xAI one point two five billion dollars per month for compute services. That's fifteen billion dollars annualized, which gives us a real sense of how expensive it's becoming to train and run state-of-the-art AI models. It also shows that even competitors in the AI space are willing to buy from each other when the economics make sense.

This all matters because the SpaceX IPO is about to reshape how we think about Musk's sprawling empire. Investors are getting their first real window into the financials of SpaceX, and they're seeing a company that's not just a rocket maker anymore—it's a diversified tech and energy play with massive bets on AI, Bitcoin, and infrastructure. The question everyone's watching now is whether the market will value that diversity as a strength or worry about the concentration of control under Musk and the capital intensity of projects like xAI.

Turning to the broader AI ecosystem, Nvidia just posted another record quarter, but with a caveat. The chip giant announced record revenue after market close, making it clear they're still the default supplier of processing power for the AI boom. However, they also signaled that revenue growth will slow in the following quarter. Investors had been bracing for this—Nvidia can't grow at two-hundred percent forever—but the forward guidance matters because it'll shape how the market values the whole AI infrastructure play going forward.

There's also something else in Nvidia's filing that caught attention. The company revealed it's holding forty-three billion dollars in startup holdings. That's a massive venture portfolio, effectively making Nvidia one of the largest venture investors in the world. Those are portfolio companies that are betting on everything from new chip designs to AI applications to infrastructure startups. Nvidia's dominance in chips has given them both the cash and the strategic insight to invest across the entire ecosystem. It's a way to hedge against disruption while also getting exposure to the next wave of innovation.

Bitcoin miners are having a good week because of all this. As Nvidia posted its blowout earnings and strong outlook, miners tied to AI infrastructure have been rising. There's a logic to it—AI is consuming enormous amounts of electricity, and if you own energy infrastructure or mining operations, you benefit from any infrastructure buildout. The correlation between AI capex cycles and mining activity is becoming increasingly important to watch.

In other news, a startup called Clouted just raised seven million dollars in a seed round to tackle a specific problem in the creator economy. The company wants to take the guesswork out of making short videos go viral. Their pitch is that they can identify which clips from longer videos have the best chance of going viral on platforms like TikTok and YouTube Shorts. Slow Ventures led the round. There's a real business here because creators are drowning in their own content—they shoot hours of material but only release a fraction of it. If you can programmatically identify the moments that are most likely to resonate, that's genuinely useful and could save creators huge amounts of time. It's also the kind of problem that's becoming increasingly important as the volume of video content explodes.

Now let's turn to crypto policy, where there's been real movement on several fronts. The Federal Reserve has proposed allowing limited master accounts for crypto firms. This has been a long-standing ask from the industry because having a direct account at the Fed would solve a lot of the banking infrastructure problems that crypto companies have struggled with. Instead of needing to go through traditional banks that are often reluctant to serve crypto clients, crypto firms could theoretically have more direct access to payment systems. It's not a full embrace of the industry, but it's a meaningful step toward integrating crypto into the traditional financial infrastructure.

Polymarket, the prediction market platform, is expanding in a different direction. They're moving to list parlays while the SEC is simultaneously seeking public input on prediction market ETFs. Polymarket's been one of the most successful applications of prediction market technology, letting users bet on everything from elections to economic outcomes to celebrity gossip. Parlays would let users chain multiple bets together, similar to sports betting. Meanwhile, the SEC looking for public input on prediction market ETFs suggests they're seriously considering whether to approve exchange-traded funds based on these platforms. That would be a huge mainstream validation for the whole prediction market space.

On the tax side, Congress is taking another swing at crypto policy. The latest proposal would direct the IRS to review de minimis exemptions—essentially the threshold below which crypto transactions aren't taxable. Right now, the existing rules are pretty murky, and Congress is trying to tighten things up. How they set those thresholds will have real implications for whether casual users need to track every single transaction or whether there are reasonable exemptions for small trades.

There's also been movement on institutional crypto infrastructure. Securitize, which tokenizes real-world assets, just had a record quarter and is planning to go public. However, they remain unprofitable, which tells you something about the state of that sector. The company is growing fast and clearly sees a path to a public listing, but they haven't figured out how to turn that growth into positive cash flow yet. That's the story for a lot of infrastructure plays—they're essential to the ecosystem but monetizing them is harder than expected.

That's what's moving the markets this Wednesday. The big story is SpaceX's IPO filing giving us a rare peek at Elon Musk's financial empire, from the billions xAI is burning to the fifteen-billion-dollar annual deal with Anthropic. Nvidia's still crushing it, though growth is moderating. And on the crypto side, we're seeing meaningful progress on regulation and infrastructure even as companies like Securitize race to go public while still unprofitable. There's a lot more volatility coming as these pieces play out, so stay tuned.
